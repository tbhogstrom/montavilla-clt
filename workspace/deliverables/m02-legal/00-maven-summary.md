## Critical Takeaways

### 1. Independent Formation Is the Only Viable Path

Proud Ground's mission is exclusively residential homeownership. They cannot hold a $5M commercial property without jeopardizing their own 501(c)(3). This is not a judgment call — it's a structural incompatibility.

**What this means:** We form our own Oregon nonprofit public benefit corporation. Proud Ground stays involved as a technical advisor and letter-of-support partner.

### 2. The Tax Exemption Story Changes

The prospectus references CLT property tax benefits, and the community expects them. Statton's research shows the statute people usually cite (ORS 307.540-548) is a **low-income housing exemption** that flat-out doesn't apply to a farmers market and community hub.

The viable path is ORS 307.130 (general charitable institution exemption), but it requires careful structuring — especially around commercial tenants like Portland Guitar Repair. Best case is full exemption ($0 tax). Realistic base case is partial exemption with ~$15k-20k/year in property taxes.

**What this means for Ledger:** Financial models must budget $15k-20k/year property tax as the base case, not $0. Full exemption is the upside scenario, not the assumption.

### 3. The 27-Month Clock Is Ticking

Once we file Articles of Incorporation, we have 27 months to file IRS Form 1023 and get retroactive 501(c)(3) status. We can accept donations during the pendency period, but every week we delay filing shrinks the window.

Form 1023 (not 1023-EZ) is required — our asset base will exceed $250k at acquisition. Current IRS processing: 3-6 months.
