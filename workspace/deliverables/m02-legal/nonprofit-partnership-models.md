## 1. Fiscal Sponsorship

### What It Is

A fiscal sponsor is an existing 501(c)(3) nonprofit that agrees to receive and administer tax-deductible donations on behalf of a project that doesn't yet have its own tax-exempt status. The sponsor holds the funds, provides financial oversight, and ensures IRS compliance. The project operates under the sponsor's legal umbrella.

There are two main models:

- **Model A (Comprehensive):** The project is a program of the sponsor. The sponsor has legal control over the funds and the project. Staff are employees of the sponsor. This is the most common model and the one most funders recognize.
- **Model C (Pre-Approved Grant):** The sponsor makes grants to the project, which operates more independently. Less common, requires more trust, but preserves more project autonomy.

### What It Typically Costs

- **Administrative fee:** 5-10% of all funds received. Industry standard is around 7%.
- Some sponsors charge flat monthly fees instead of or in addition to percentages.
- Setup fees range from $0 to $2,000 depending on the organization.

### What the Sponsor Typically Requires

- A project description and budget
- Named project director with clear accountability
- Regular financial reporting (monthly or quarterly)
- Alignment with the sponsor's mission (this is where it gets tricky for us — see below)
- Agreement on decision-making authority (who controls fund disbursement?)
- Insurance, depending on activities

## 2. CLT Technical Assistance Programs

### Grounded Solutions Network (formerly National Community Land Trust Network)

- The national membership and TA organization for community land trusts, shared equity, and community-controlled housing.
- Based in Portland, OR (which is convenient for us).
- Offers:
  - **Startup technical assistance** for emerging CLTs — organizational development, governance design, ground lease structuring
  - **Training and peer learning** — national conferences, webinars, resource library
  - **Policy advocacy** at federal and state levels
  - **Research and data** on CLT best practices
- Membership is available to emerging CLTs, not just established ones.
- Website: groundedsolutions.org
- **Action:** Apply for membership and request startup TA consultation. They are literally headquartered in Portland. This should be our first call.

### Urban Land Conservancy (ULC)

- Denver-based land bank and CLT capacity builder with national reach.
- Acquires and holds land for community benefit, then partners with local organizations for development.
- Provides technical assistance to emerging CLTs, including governance, financing, and community engagement.
- Has a track record of supporting early-stage CLT efforts and helping them get to organizational viability.
- Their Transit-Oriented Development Fund model could be relevant if we pursue mixed-use.
- Website: urbanlandc.org
- **Action:** See the sponsorship pitch (separate deliverable). Requesting introductory call.

### Enterprise Community Partners

- National community development intermediary. Major funder and TA provider for affordable housing and community development.
- Pacific Northwest office based in Seattle.
- Programs relevant to us:
  - **Enterprise Community Development** — TA for community-based organizations
  - **Rose Fellowship** — places architecture/design fellows in community organizations (long shot but interesting)
  - **Grant programs** — various, typically for established organizations but worth monitoring
- Website: enterprisecommunity.org
- Relevance: More useful at a later stage when we have legal standing and a clearer project scope. Not a day-4 resource, but should be on our radar for months 3-6.

### Lincoln Institute of Land Policy

- Cambridge-based think tank that studies land policy, including CLTs extensively.
- Publishes key CLT research and has funded CLT technical assistance.
- Not a direct TA provider for individual CLTs, but their publications are essential reading and they convene practitioners.
- Website: lincolninst.edu
- Relevance: Background research, not direct assistance.

### TA Programs: Bottom Line

**Grounded Solutions Network is the clear first move.** They're in Portland, they exist specifically to help CLTs like us get started, and membership gives us access to a national peer network. ULC is our second move (already in progress with the sponsorship pitch). Enterprise is a later-stage resource.

## 3. Affiliate / Chapter Model: Operating Under Proud Ground

### What This Would Look Like

Proud Ground is Portland's established community land trust, founded in 1999. They hold ground leases on 300+ permanently affordable homes across the metro area. They have a board, staff, legal infrastructure, 501(c)(3) status, and deep relationships with the City of Portland, Metro, Oregon Housing and Community Services, and CDFIs like Craft3 and Community Vision.

An affiliate or chapter model would mean MCLT operates as a program or sub-entity of Proud Ground, using their legal standing, financial systems, and organizational credibility.

### Pros

- **Instant credibility.** Proud Ground is respected. Operating under their umbrella answers the "who are you?" question immediately.
- **Legal standing on day one.** No need to incorporate, file for 501(c)(3), or find a fiscal sponsor. We'd be part of an existing nonprofit.
- **Ground lease expertise.** Proud Ground has 25+ years of ground lease experience. We wouldn't need to reinvent the wheel.
- **Financial infrastructure.** Accounting, auditing, fund management, grant compliance — all handled.
- **Funder relationships.** Proud Ground already has relationships with the funders we'd be approaching. Their endorsement opens doors.
- **Staff support.** Depending on the arrangement, we might have access to Proud Ground staff for organizational development.

### Cons

- **Loss of autonomy.** As a Proud Ground program, our decisions would ultimately be subject to their board's approval. If the community decides on an outcome Proud Ground's board doesn't support, we have a problem.
- **Mission mismatch.** Proud Ground's mission is permanently affordable homeownership. Our initiative is outcome-open by design. If the community decides this lot should be a park, or a market, or a mixed-use building with commercial space and no housing component, that may not fit within Proud Ground's organizational mission.
- **Framing risk.** Operating under a housing CLT signals "housing" to the community and to funders, even if we say we're outcome-open. This directly undermines our strategic reframe.
- **Organizational culture.** Proud Ground is a professional, staff-driven organization. We're a grassroots, volunteer-driven initiative. Those cultures can clash.
- **Exit complexity.** If we start under Proud Ground and later want to become independent, disentangling could be messy — especially if funds were raised under their EIN.

### A Middle Path

Rather than a full affiliate model, we could pursue a **technical assistance agreement** with Proud Ground:
- They advise us on CLT mechanics, governance, and ground lease design
- We remain organizationally independent
- We credit their advisory role publicly (credibility boost without legal entanglement)
- We use a different fiscal sponsor (Community Vision, ULC) for financial operations

This gets us Proud Ground's expertise and partial credibility without locking us into their institutional framework or mission constraints.

### Proud Ground: Bottom Line

**Full affiliate model is not recommended** given our outcome-open strategy. The mission mismatch is real and would create problems the moment the community process surfaces non-housing outcomes. A **technical assistance relationship** is the right level of engagement. Save the formal partnership conversation for after the community has spoken on land use direction.

## 4. Independent Formation

### Oregon Nonprofit Incorporation

- **File Articles of Incorporation** with the Oregon Secretary of State.
- Filing fee: $50 online.
- Processing time: Typically 1-3 business days for online filing.
- You immediately have a legal Oregon nonprofit corporation. You can open a bank account, sign contracts, and receive donations (though not yet tax-deductible).
- Website: sos.oregon.gov/business

### Federal 501(c)(3) Application

- **File IRS Form 1023 or 1023-EZ** for tax-exempt status.
- Form 1023-EZ (simplified): Available for organizations expecting less than $50,000 in annual gross receipts and less than $250,000 in total assets. Filing fee: $275. We likely qualify in year one.
- Form 1023 (full): Required if we exceed the thresholds or want to be thorough. Filing fee: $600.
- Processing time: **3-6 months** for 1023-EZ. **6-12 months** for full 1023. IRS backlogs vary.
- Once approved, tax-exempt status is retroactive to the date of incorporation (or the date of the application if filed within 27 months of incorporation).

### Oregon Charitable Activities Registration

- Required for any nonprofit soliciting donations in Oregon.
- Register with the Oregon Department of Justice, Charitable Activities Section.
- Filing fee: $10.
- Must be done before soliciting donations.
- Website: doj.state.or.us/charitable-activities

### EIN (Employer Identification Number)

- Apply online via IRS website. Free. Issued immediately.
- Needed to open a bank account and file the 501(c)(3) application.

### Timeline Summary

| Step | Time | Cost |
|------|------|------|
| Draft Articles of Incorporation + Bylaws | 1-2 weeks (with attorney review) | Attorney fees or pro bono |
| File Articles with Oregon SOS | 1-3 days | $50 |
| Obtain EIN | Same day | Free |
| Register with OR DOJ Charitable Activities | 1-2 weeks | $10 |
| Open bank account | 1-2 weeks | Varies |
| File 501(c)(3) application (1023-EZ) | 3-6 months for determination | $275 |
| File 501(c)(3) application (full 1023) | 6-12 months for determination | $600 |

**Total cost for independent formation: roughly $335-$660 plus attorney fees.**

### Can We Do Fiscal Sponsorship AND File Independently in Parallel?

**Yes.** This is common and recommended. Here's how it works:

1. Secure a fiscal sponsor now (week 1-2). Begin accepting tax-deductible donations under the sponsor's EIN.
2. Incorporate as an Oregon nonprofit (week 2-3). This gives us legal entity status for contracts and non-tax-deductible activities.
3. File for 501(c)(3) (month 1-2). Begin the IRS process.
4. When 501(c)(3) is approved (month 4-12), transition from fiscal sponsorship to independent operation. Transfer remaining funds from sponsor.
5. Terminate fiscal sponsorship agreement.

Most fiscal sponsors expect this trajectory for projects that intend to become independent nonprofits. The sponsorship agreement should include a clear termination and fund-transfer clause.

## 5. Recommendation

Given our situation — urgent timeline, no legal entity, outcome-open strategy, 7 signers, $1,175 in pledges — here is the recommended path:

### Immediate (This Week)

1. **Contact Grounded Solutions Network** for startup technical assistance. They're in Portland. This is the single highest-leverage call we can make for CLT-specific guidance.

2. **Send the ULC sponsorship pitch.** Even if ULC can't do fiscal sponsorship for a Portland project, their technical assistance and strategic advice would be valuable.

3. **Contact Community Vision Capital & Consulting** about fiscal sponsorship. They're Portland-based, they understand community development broadly (not just housing), and they won't box us into a predetermined outcome. Ask for an exploratory conversation.

4. **Contact Proud Ground** — but frame it as requesting a technical assistance relationship, not a merger or affiliate arrangement. We want their expertise without their institutional constraints.

### Next 2-3 Weeks

5. **Secure a fiscal sponsor.** Community Vision or ULC, whichever responds positively first. If neither works, Proud Ground as fallback (with clear boundaries documented).

6. **Draft Articles of Incorporation and Bylaws.** We need a CLT-experienced attorney. Options:
   - Pro bono through the Oregon State Bar's Lawyer Referral Service
   - Lewis & Clark Law School Small Business Legal Clinic
   - Portland-area attorneys with CLT/nonprofit experience (ask Grounded Solutions and Proud Ground for referrals)
   - We need tripartite governance baked into the bylaws from the start (CLT standard: resident/community/public board seats)

7. **File for Oregon nonprofit incorporation.** $50, done in days.

8. **Obtain EIN.** Free, same day.

9. **Register with Oregon DOJ.** $10.

### Month 2-3

10. **File 501(c)(3) application** (1023-EZ if we qualify). Begin the IRS clock.

11. **Continue operating under fiscal sponsor** while IRS application is pending.

### The Fastest Path to Legal Standing

The absolute fastest route is:

**Week 1:** Fiscal sponsor secured (accept donations, apply for grants under their EIN).
**Week 2-3:** Oregon nonprofit incorporated (sign contracts, open bank account, operate as a legal entity).
**Month 4-8:** 501(c)(3) approved (full independent tax-exempt status).

This parallel approach gives us functional legal standing within 1-2 weeks while we build toward full independence over 4-8 months.

### What NOT to Do

- **Don't become a Proud Ground affiliate.** Not because Proud Ground is bad — they're great at what they do. But their housing mission conflicts with our outcome-open strategy, and operating under their umbrella will signal to the community that we've already decided on housing. The whole point of our reframe is that we haven't.

- **Don't wait for 501(c)(3) before doing anything.** That's 4-12 months we don't have. Fiscal sponsorship exists precisely for this situation.

- **Don't accept donations without legal cover.** If someone writes a check to "Montavilla Community Land Trust" and we have no legal entity and no fiscal sponsor, that's a mess. Get the fiscal sponsor first, then actively solicit.
