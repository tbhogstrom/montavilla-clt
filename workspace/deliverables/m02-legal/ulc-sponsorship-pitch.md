Dear ULC Team,

I'm writing to introduce the Montavilla Community Land Trust initiative and to explore whether ULC would consider a fiscal sponsorship arrangement, technical assistance partnership, or some combination as we stand up a new community land trust in Southeast Portland.

I know you get a lot of cold outreach. I'll keep this short and honest about where we are.

## The Situation

A 0.84-acre lot at 7700 SE Stark Street in Portland's Montavilla neighborhood is listed for sale at $4.999 million. The lot currently hosts the Montavilla Farmers Market (Sundays, May through November) and a small business tenant, Portland Guitar Repair. The owner is an absentee investor based in Michigan.

If a speculative developer buys this parcel, the neighborhood loses its weekly market, a small business loses its home, and the community loses any say in what gets built on a central site zoned CM2 (four-story mixed-use eligible).

A group of Montavilla residents — myself included — launched a community land trust initiative on April 12 to explore whether the neighborhood can acquire this land and hold it in permanent community trust.

## Our Approach: Community-First, Outcome-Open

We spent our first 48 hours getting real community feedback. We posted to Reddit, showed up at the Saturday market, and listened. The response was blunt: Portland needs housing, $5M is too much, and who are you to decide what goes here?

That feedback was a gift. On April 13, our steering group approved a strategic reframe. We are not campaigning to "save this lot for a farmers market." We are building a coalition so the neighborhood decides what happens here — whether that's a community commons, affordable housing, mixed-use development, a relocated market, or something none of us have thought of yet.

This is what a community land trust actually is: democratic community control over land use. We want to do it right, which means not predetermining the outcome before the community has been consulted.

## What We've Built (in 4 Days)

We are early. I won't pretend otherwise. But we've moved fast:

- **Campaign website** with petition system, pledge ledger, and email infrastructure (live, deployed on Vercel with Neon database backend)
- **Published prospectus** outlining the CLT model, capital stack scenarios, and governance framework
- **Community feedback analysis** — 78 Reddit comments systematically analyzed, informing our strategic pivot
- **Approved strategy document** — the community-first, outcome-open reframe, with clear implementation plan
- **7 petition signatures and $1,175 in pledges** — small numbers, but real people with real commitments four days in
- **Identified steering committee candidates** from signup data (skills-based: legal, finance, organizing, communications)

What we don't have: a legal entity, fiscal sponsorship, retained counsel, or formalized partnerships with Proud Ground (Portland's established CLT) or the Montavilla Neighborhood Association. Those are the gaps we're working to close right now.

## Why ULC

I've followed ULC's work for a while. You don't just talk about community land trusts — you acquire land, hold it, and build the organizational capacity so communities can steward it long-term. Your Transit-Oriented Development Fund, your partnerships with emerging CLTs across the country, your technical assistance work — that's exactly the kind of infrastructure and expertise we need.

Portland doesn't have a robust CLT ecosystem yet. Proud Ground does important work in permanently affordable homeownership, but their model is housing-specific. Our initiative is outcome-open by design. That's a different animal, and we think it could be a meaningful addition to the CLT landscape in the Pacific Northwest — if we build it on solid footing.

Here's what I think ULC could offer, and I'm open to your read on what makes sense:

1. **Fiscal sponsorship** — We need the ability to accept tax-deductible donations, apply for grants, and sign contracts while we pursue independent 501(c)(3) status. A fiscal sponsorship arrangement with ULC would give us immediate legal standing and associate our work with a credible national organization.

2. **Technical assistance** — Governance structure, ground lease design, capital stack architecture, community engagement process design. We have energy and community buy-in for the process. We need experienced guidance on the CLT mechanics.

3. **Strategic advice** — You've seen dozens of CLTs at this stage. What works? What kills early-stage efforts? Where should we focus our limited energy?

## Why Now

The property is listed. We don't know how long it will sit — expert opinion from an affordable housing developer in the Reddit thread suggests no one will pay $5M and the price will drop — but we can't count on time we don't control. Every week without legal standing is a week we can't accept grants, can't formalize partnerships, and can't engage the seller's broker as a serious party.
