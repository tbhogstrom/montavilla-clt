### Fiscal Sponsor Pathway — Our Route to Eligibility

PCEF explicitly allows unincorporated community groups to apply through fiscal sponsors. Key rules:

- **Sponsor IS the formal grantee** — signs the grant agreement, is legally responsible for funds and deliverables
- **MCLT submits the application** with sponsor information included
- **Sponsor fee:** up to 10% of grant budget ($20K on a $200K grant)
- **Combined overhead + sponsor fee:** cannot exceed 25% of requested funds
- **Sponsor must provide:** EIN, 3 years of financial statements, financial management descriptions
- **If sponsor not yet secured at time of drafting:** Can enter "N/A" in Tax ID field — but this MUST be resolved before submission

## 3. ELIGIBLE ACTIVITIES — WHAT FITS OUR $200K SCOPE

### The $100K vs $200K Question — CRITICAL STRATEGIC DECISION

PCEF distinguishes two planning grant tiers:

| Type | Cap | What Counts |
|------|-----|-------------|
| **Technical planning** | $200,000 | Feasibility studies, energy modeling, engineering, site assessments |
| **Non-technical planning** | $100,000 | Community engagement, general research, curriculum development |

**Our strategy: Frame as TECHNICAL planning with community engagement as an essential methodology within it.** If we lead with community engagement, we're capped at $100K. If we lead with site assessment, feasibility study, and engineering analysis — with community engagement as how we conduct that technical work — we access the $200K cap.

This is not a stretch. A CLT feasibility study for a 0.84-acre site absolutely requires technical planning: environmental assessment, infrastructure analysis, zoning and development feasibility, cost modeling, and engineering/design concepts. The community engagement is the process by which we determine what to plan for.

**ACTION NEEDED:** Submit a question to PCEF via their online form asking whether a community land trust feasibility study qualifies as "technical planning." Response time: 5-7 business days. Must submit by May 5 at latest.

**What earns full points:**
- Organization's PRIMARY PURPOSE is serving PCEF priority populations (people of color, low-income, people with disabilities)
- This is the MAJORITY of their work
- Team has relevant professional AND/OR life experience (life experience valued equally)
- Strong community engagement track record
- "Innate or personal understanding" of served populations

**Our challenge:** MCLT has no organizational track record. We're brand new.

**How to maximize:**
1. **Fiscal sponsor's track record covers organizational credibility.** Choose a sponsor with demonstrated PCEF priority population service. Verde or Rosewood Initiative would score highest here.
2. **Team bios must shine.** Every coalition member needs a bio emphasizing: (a) connection to Montavilla neighborhood, (b) professional or life experience relevant to community development, food access, or land stewardship, (c) racial/ethnic/income identity where appropriate and voluntary.
3. **Frame MCLT as an emerging coalition** — PCEF explicitly values "community-powered" and "building capacity in emerging groups." Our newness is a feature if framed correctly: this is exactly what planning grants are for.
4. **40 petition signatures and 20 member signups** demonstrate existing community engagement. Mention these as evidence of grassroots traction.
5. **National CLT outreach and Proud Ground ED meeting** show we're embedded in the CLT ecosystem, not operating in isolation.

**Estimated score if we execute well:** 18-22 out of 30 (partial — our biggest point gap, but recoverable).

### Project Plan & Benefits (55 pts) — OUR STRONGEST CATEGORY

**Planning grants are scored on a simplified rubric (5 sub-criteria instead of the full implementation rubric):**

**4a. Opportunity Statement & Climate Justice (subset of 55)**
- Full points: Clear demonstration of climate justice for PCEF priority populations; "firm understanding" of issue; explanation of how planning grant addresses opportunity.
- **Our play:** The 82nd Avenue corridor is a documented environmental justice area. Montavilla has priority populations (low-income, communities of color). An auto dealership lot is impervious surface generating stormwater runoff, urban heat island effects, and zero food access. Converting to community-owned green space with regenerative agriculture directly addresses climate justice.

**4b. Planning Scope Clarity & Feasibility (subset of 55)**
- Full points: Clear planning outcomes; process "centers equity and justice"; appropriate identified partners; "well described" engagement/outreach; identified research/analysis needs; barriers identified with mitigation plan; "reasonable" timeline.
- **Our play:** We have a specific site (7700 SE Stark), a clear planning scope (CLT feasibility + site assessment + community engagement), identified partners (fiscal sponsor, Proud Ground TA, Grounded Solutions Network), and a participatory methodology. The 2-year timeline is generous for our scope. Identify barriers honestly: property access, community trust, organizational capacity — and show mitigation for each.

**4c. Project Beneficiary — Priority Population Focus (subset of 55)**
- Full points: "Highly likely" beneficiaries are PCEF priority population members.
- **Our play:** Montavilla demographics + 82nd Ave corridor proximity. Food access benefits flow to low-income residents. Green space benefits flow to environmental justice communities. Community ownership model is explicitly anti-displacement.

**4d. PCEF Guiding Principles Alignment (subset of 55)**
- Full points: Alignment with "focused on climate action with multiple benefits, community powered, justice driven, and accountable."
- **Our play:** This is literally our strategy — community-powered, outcome-open, with climate benefits baked in. Quote the PCEF principles and show direct alignment.

**Estimated score if we execute well:** 38-45 out of 55 (strong).

## 7. RED FLAGS & COMPLIANCE RISKS

### Risk 1: Organizational Track Record (HIGH)

**The problem:** MCLT doesn't exist as a legal entity. We have no organizational track record, no financial history, no prior grants. The Organization & Project Team section is worth 30 points — our weakest area.

**Mitigation:**
- Choose a fiscal sponsor with strong PCEF track record (Verde, Rosewood Initiative)
- Lead with coalition members' individual experience and community roots
- Frame the planning grant as organizational development — that's literally what it funds
- PCEF explicitly commits to "building capacity in emerging groups"

### Risk 2: Financial Review Scrutiny (MEDIUM-HIGH)

**The problem:** PCEF applies "additional scrutiny" when the grant exceeds 2x the applicant's average annual revenue or represents a new area of work. For a brand-new org, $200K is infinitely more than our revenue (which is $0).

**Mitigation:** The fiscal sponsor's financials are what get reviewed, not ours. A sponsor with $500K+ annual revenue makes the $200K grant look proportionate. **This is why sponsor selection matters so much — it's not just eligibility, it's scoring.**

### Risk 3: The $200K Technical vs. $100K Non-Technical Line (MEDIUM)

**The problem:** If reviewers classify our work as primarily community engagement, we could be capped at $100K or the application could be flagged during eligibility screening.

**Mitigation:**
- Budget allocates 29% ($58K) explicitly to technical activities
- Lead the project description with technical assessment scope
- Community engagement is framed as methodology within the technical planning
- Submit a clarifying question to PCEF before May 5

### Risk 4: Property Access Not Secured (MEDIUM)

**The problem:** The site is privately owned and listed for $5M. We don't control access.

**Mitigation:** PCEF FAQ states "site access does not need to be fully confirmed...applicants should demonstrate that access can reasonably be secured during the project period." We can demonstrate:
- Owner's trust rep (Adam) is open to community solutions (warm intro via Co-op/METBA)
- Property is overpriced and likely to sit — extended timeline works in our favor
- Planning work (community engagement, org development, scenario planning) can proceed without site access
- Site-specific technical work (environmental assessment) requires only temporary access permission

**ACTION:** Get a letter from Adam (or at least a documented conversation) indicating openness to the community process. This doesn't need to be a commitment — just a signal.

### Risk 5: GHG Reduction Narrative Must Be Credible (LOW-MEDIUM)

**The problem:** PCEF exists to reduce greenhouse gas emissions. A community land trust / farmers market project is not a solar panel installation — the GHG connection requires careful framing.

**Mitigation:**
- Urban agriculture sequesters carbon in soil (well-documented)
- Green infrastructure (bioswales, rain gardens, depaving) manages stormwater and reduces heat island
- Preserving open/green space prevents emissions from new commercial/residential construction
- Permanent farmers market reduces food-miles transportation emissions for the neighborhood
- PCEF staff calculate GHG estimates — we provide the data inputs, they do the math

### Risk 6: WCEA Compliance for a Planning Grant (LOW)

**The problem:** Workforce & Contractor Equity Agreement requirements apply to ALL projects. For a planning grant, this means our hiring of consultants and contractors must demonstrate diverse recruitment.

**Mitigation:** Proactively identify BIPOC-owned environmental, engineering, and legal firms in Portland for the contracted work. Budget for this from day one. 5 points is 5 points.

### Risk 7: Reporting and Compliance Burden (LOW)

**The problem:** Quarterly performance and financial reporting required. For a volunteer-heavy org with no staff, this could become a burden.

**Mitigation:** Budget for reporting coordination. Fiscal sponsor handles financial reporting. Project coordinator handles performance reporting. PCEF covers platform costs and provides training.

### Risk 8: Post-Award Right-Sizing (INFO)

PCEF will not partially fund applications — projects are awarded or rejected in full. However, they may discuss "right-sizing" before award. If they think $200K is too much, they'll say so. Be prepared to defend every line item.
