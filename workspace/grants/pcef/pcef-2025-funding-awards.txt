Climate Investment Plan and Community Grants

The Climate Investment Plan (CIP) is the Portland Clean Energy Community Benefits Fund (PCEF) five-
year plan that guides the program's investments. The CIP includes 37 Strategic Programs and the
Community Grants program. All programs in the CIP are evaluated within PCEF's equity and climate
framework, which considers benefits to frontline communities, community leadership, implementation
feasibility, greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions reductions, and accountability.

The 2025 Community Grants funding cycle is part of the inaugural CIP and is aimed at supporting
nonprofit-led, planning, and implementation projects that reduce GHGs and improve climate resilience
equitably. The CIP includes five-year funding allocations for each of PCEF's funding areas for the
Community Grants program. Below are details of each of the funding areas, their five-year Community
Grants allocations in the CIP, and examples of the types of projects that are eligible for funding.

              Renewable Energy and Energy Efficiency

                  Five-year Community Grants funding allocation: $156,000,000
                  Energy efficiency and renewable energy-focused Community Grants are intended to
                  capture investments in:

    · Targeted/culturally responsive single-family home clean energy upgrades.
    · Clean energy upgrades in existing regulated multifamily affordable housing.
    · Clean energy improvements in nonprofit owned/occupied commercial buildings.
    · Community solar.
    · Other renewable energy and energy efficiency investments that equitably reduce GHG

         emissions and will not be served by a strategic program defined in the CIP.

               Transportation Decarbonization

                   Five-year Community Grants funding allocation: $59,000,000
                   Transportation decarbonization, the newest funding category for PCEF, is intended to
                   capture investments in:

    · Programming, including education and outreach, that expands access to and promotes
         sustainable transportation modes. These include walking, biking, public transportation,
         carpooling, and electric vehicle usage.

    · Electric vehicle car share program at an affordable housing site or bike-sharing program at a
         community center.

    · Switching an organization's high-mileage, shared vehicles to electric vehicles (e.g., vans for after
         school programs serving low-income youth or community transportation services for priority
         populations).

    · Charging infrastructure that is equitable, convenient, reliable, affordable, safe, and accessible.
    · Community-centered transportation planning processes that focus on the needs of priority

         populations.
    · Other transportation decarbonization projects that equitably reduce GHG emissions and will not

         be served by a strategic program defined in the CIP.

Outreach and applicant support

PCEF staff hosted 7 virtual information and office hour sessions during regular work hours, evenings, and
weekends to answer questions from prospective applicants. More than 180 individuals attended PCEF-
hosted application support sessions. Staff and contracted technical assistance providers delivered
trainings focused on grant writing and budget development; multifamily, commercial, and single-family
residential energy efficiency and renewable energy; workforce and contractor development, and
regenerative agriculture and green infrastructure. These sessions were recorded and uploaded to the
PCEF YouTube account and have collectively received more than 400 video views.

Additionally, staff contracted with consultants to provide an additional 25 meetings where they
provided grant writing and budget development support, prioritizing small and emerging organizations,
and energy efficiency consultations to help inform and support project proposals. The funding
application period opened on March 26, 2025, and closed on May 27, 2025.

Types of grants

There were two types of grants available: Planning Grants and Implementation Grants. Planning Grants
support organizations in conducting assessments or planning needed to develop a full project proposal,
with eligible activities including research and learning, feasibility evaluations, community outreach,
partnership building, and curriculum development.

Implementation Grants were designed for projects that advance economic, social, and climate justice,
including physical improvements like weatherization and planting trees and non-physical activities like
workforce training. These funds could be used for staff time, contracted work, purchasing equipment,
materials, and supplies, fulfilling program requirements, and other project needs.

The 2025 Community Grants funding cycle also offered general operating support as the second year of
a pilot program, available for eligible organizations whose primary focus is local food production using
regenerative agricultural practices. The amount covered is capped at 25% of operating costs or $200,000
annually, whichever is less. This general operating support is for three-year grants and applies only to
organizations that pay their entire staff wages at or above the PCEF minimum wage.

Application review process

The application review process included eligibility screening, technical review, financial review, and
reference checks. Scoring panels then reviewed all eligible applications to determine the recommended
portfolio. Each of these steps is outlined below.

Eligibility screening

Program staff reviewed each application to ensure the organization and project are eligible for funding.
Eligibility criteria are informed by PCEF legislation as well as City contracting and legal requirements. All
applications must be submitted by eligible nonprofits for climate mitigation and adaptation projects
within one of the PCEF funding areas: energy efficiency, renewable energy, transportation

Applicant vetting

All eligible applicants underwent a vetting process that included reference checks and a review of
organizational financials. Applications that raised questions during preliminary review, or that requested
funding exceeding twice their average annual revenue or proposed work outside the organization's prior
experience, received additional scrutiny.

Reference checks. Staff requested up to three external references to verify an applicant's prior work,
timeliness, and successful completion of projects comparable in scale and complexity to the proposed
effort. References were asked to comment on the organization's ability to carry out projects effectively
and to note any concerns about its capacity.

Financial review. The financial review examined the past three years of financial statements and related
narrative responses in the application to assess the organization's financial stability and governance.
Newer organizations with fewer than three years of records were required to submit all available
financial documentation.

Scoring criteria

Applications were scored on criteria specific to their project type. Applications that included a workforce
and contractor development (WCD) component, along with implementation of energy efficiency,

PCEF 2025 Community Grants Funding Awards                 Page 8 of 81
renewable energy, green infrastructure, regenerative agriculture, and/or transportation decarbonization
components were scored on all relevant criteria, with scores weighted based on the share of the budget
allocated to each area of work. The scoring criteria includes weights so that different criteria impact the
final score to a different extent, based on prioritizations that were established through community
input. Each application had an opportunity to receive up to 100 points. PCEF staff made all scoring
criteria and information publicly available online and encouraged applicants to review the materials
thoroughly when preparing their applications.

Scoring panel evaluations

Applications that passed eligibility screening and technical review were assigned to a scoring panel. Each
panel included three members drawn from PCEF Committee members, program staff, community
representatives, and subject matter experts, ensuring diverse perspectives and relevant expertise. Staff
reviewed potential conflicts of interest when forming panels, and all panelists completed a conflict-of-
interest disclosure form and received training on scoring criteria and bias awareness. Panelists were
instructed not to communicate with applicants during the review process. The scoring process included
five steps:

    1. Each reviewer independently scored applications using the provided criteria.
    2. PCEF staff compiled the individual draft scores and calculated the average for each criterion.
    3. Panels met to discuss scores and applicant responses, after which reviewers could adjust their

         ratings. A final average score was then assigned to each application.
    4. Final scores were weighted based on the scoring criteria, and PCEF staff added a greenhouse gas

         (GHG) impact score to produce the overall score.
    5. Applications were required to meet a minimum score threshold to advance for funding

         consideration.

Recommended portfolio development

The recommended portfolio was developed based on several factors, including application type, ranking
of application scores, funding allocations for each program area, and overall proposal strength. Several
projects were awarded partial funding based on evaluation of implementation feasibility and eligibility
of measures.

Partial funding decisions were based on considerations such as an organization's capacity to complete
the project, inclusion of ineligible expenses, adjustments to project scope, and other relevant factors.
The goal of these awards is to set organizations and their projects up for success while maintaining
alignment with PCEF program requirements. PCEF staff will work with these organizations during the
grant agreement drafting process to refine project scopes and budgets, maximizing greenhouse gas
reductions and community benefits while allowing necessary adjustments.

GHG calculations

GHG reduction estimates are based on information provided by applicants on planned physical
improvements and behavior change results. When needed, supplemental values were sourced from
regional studies, adopted standards, or similar programs. Emissions reductions were conservatively
calculated and used the assumed lifetime of the measure or mix of measures for projects that include
physical infrastructure or technologies. Emissions factors were used to convert electricity and fuel
savings into CO2e values. These were generated with utility emissions factors from the Department of

Environmental Quality and annual sales from utility reporting. The electricity emissions factors were
then assumed to linearly reduce to zero by 2040, as per HB 2021, and they are adjusted to account for
line loss using values published by the Energy Information Administration.

The nine recommended planning grants represent planning efforts across all funding areas (e.g., energy
efficiency, transportation decarbonization, regenerative agriculture and green infrastructure, workforce
development, and other GHG-reducing projects).

Clean energy projects include energy efficiency and renewable energy improvements to single-family,
multifamily, and commercial properties with an emphasis on deep energy retrofits. Regenerative
agriculture and green infrastructure projects include gardening, food production, and food sovereignty.
Workforce development projects will train both existing and new workers in climate-related
technologies, trades, and practices, preparing them for jobs in the green economy. Contractor support
projects will resource and credential disadvantaged contractors to pivot towards climate-related
contracting opportunities. Transportation decarbonization projects include those focused on promoting
increased active transportation through incentives and education as well as electric vehicle (EV)
conversation of internal combustion engine fleets.
