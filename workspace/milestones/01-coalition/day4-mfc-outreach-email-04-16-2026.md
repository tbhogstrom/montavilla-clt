Four days in. I want to tell you what happened this morning.

![Screenshot of email from MFC Board](IMAGE_PLACEHOLDER — replace with actual screenshot)

That's the Montavilla Food Co-op board reaching out to us. Not the other way around. They saw the flyer at the farmers market and wanted to talk.

I want to explain why this matters more than it might look.

**The biggest criticism we've gotten — and it's fair — is that we don't have backing from established Montavilla institutions.** No market endorsement. No neighborhood association. No co-op. Just a website and seven signatures. The Reddit thread said it plainly: prove you're real before asking for money.

The MFC board reaching out unprompted is the first crack in that wall.

**Why the co-op matters:**

The Montavilla Food Co-op is a community-owned enterprise in our neighborhood. They already operate on the principle that a community can collectively own something — that's what a cooperative is. A community land trust is the same idea applied to land. If anyone in Montavilla intuitively understands what we're trying to do, it's the co-op board.

They're also the kind of organization whose involvement signals to everyone else — funders, the city, the neighborhood association, the market itself — that this isn't just one guy with a website. It's a real coalition forming around a real question: what does this neighborhood want to happen at 7700 SE Stark?

**What I'm going to do:**

I'm calling them today. I'm not going to pitch. I'm going to listen. I want to know what they think, what they're worried about, what role they'd want to play — if any. Advisory? Steering committee? Letter of support? That's their call, not mine.

I'm also going to be honest about where we are: 7 signatures, $1,175 pledged, no legal entity yet, a strategic reframe that says the community decides the outcome — not us. If that's enough to start a conversation, great. If they need to see more before getting involved, that's fair too.

**What this means for you:**

Every time someone new shows up at the table, it gets easier for the next person to show up. The co-op reaching out makes it more likely that the neighborhood association pays attention. The neighborhood association paying attention makes it more likely that the market leadership engages. That's how coalitions build — not all at once, but one conversation at a time.

We're still at 7 signatures. That number still needs to move. But today it feels like something shifted.
