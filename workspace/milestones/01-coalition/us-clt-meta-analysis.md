## Part I: The National Landscape

### 1.1 Total Count and Growth Trajectory

As of the most recent data available, there are **308 CLTs operating across 48 states, Washington D.C., and Puerto Rico** (2024 survey cited by the Lincoln Institute of Land Policy). This figure represents dramatic growth over two decades:

| Year | Number of CLTs |
|------|---------------|
| 2006 | 162 |
| 2018 | 225 |
| 2021 | 289 |
| 2022 | 314 (including shared equity entities) |
| 2024 | 308+ |

The 2022 Census (conducted by Grounded Solutions Network in partnership with the University of Toronto, published by the Lincoln Institute of Land Policy with support from Freddie Mac) is the most comprehensive study available. It surveyed 314 CLT and nonprofit shared equity entities. Of 210 active CLTs with residential units studied, **55 (26%) were incorporated after 2017**, indicating a rapid surge in recent years.

**Key source:** [2022 Census of Community Land Trusts — Lincoln Institute of Land Policy](https://www.lincolninst.edu/publications/working-papers/2022-census-community-land-trusts-shared-equity-entities-in-united/)

## Part II: CLT Typology and Models

### 2.1 The Classic CLT Structure

All CLTs share a core legal architecture:
- Nonprofit 501(c)(3) corporation holds land in perpetuity
- Long-term (typically 99-year, renewable) ground lease separates land ownership from improvements ownership
- A tripartite board: 1/3 CLT residents/leaseholders, 1/3 community members, 1/3 public interest representatives
- Resale formula limits appreciation to maintain permanent affordability while allowing leaseholders to build equity

The first CLT in the U.S., **New Communities Inc.** (Albany, Georgia, 1969), was agricultural. The first urban CLT is generally credited to **Community Land Cooperative of Cincinnati** (1981). The model's codification and spread was largely driven by the **Institute for Community Economics** (Springfield, MA), which provided a revolving loan fund and technical assistance from the late 1970s through the 1990s.

---

### 2.2 CLT Focus Area Typology

**Housing CLTs** (majority): Steward permanently affordable homeownership, rental, and cooperative housing. Examples: Champlain Housing Trust, Proud Ground, Homestead CLT.

**Agricultural CLTs**: Hold farmland and lease it to farmers, removing land cost from startup burden. Examples: New Communities Inc., Lopez Community Land Trust, Agrarian Commons network, South of the Sound Community Land Trust (140 acres, Olympia WA), Berkshire Community Land Trust.

**Commercial/Mixed-Use CLTs**: Acquire commercial corridors or mixed-use buildings to anchor small businesses and community institutions against displacement. This is the most emerging and directly relevant model for MCLT. Key examples:
- **Rondo CLT** (St. Paul, MN) — Minnesota's first commercial CLT
- **Crescent City CLT** (New Orleans, LA) — commercial corridors + housing
- **Africatown CLT** (Seattle, WA) — mixed residential/commercial/cultural
- **Urban Land Conservancy** (Denver, CO) — 300,000 sq ft commercial/nonprofit space preserved
- **Partnership in Property** (Minneapolis, MN) — BIPOC small business focus
- **Dudley Neighbors Inc.** (Boston, MA) — urban farm, greenhouse, community facilities

**Hybrid/Multi-Use CLTs**: The majority of mature CLTs combine two or more of the above. Lopez Community Land Trust (Lopez Island, WA) is the clearest Pacific Northwest example: 47 cooperatively owned homes + 100 acres of farmland.

### 3.3 Selected National CLTs of Particular Relevance to MCLT

#### Large-Scale Housing CLT Models

**Champlain Housing Trust** (Burlington, Vermont)
- Founded: 1984 (as Burlington Community Land Trust); merged 2006
- Scale: 2,600+ affordable apartments, 675 shared equity homes/condos, 150 staff
- Budget: $28M operating + $20M property operations + $60M in annual development
- The largest CLT in the U.S. Received a $20M donation from MacKenzie Scott in 2023
- Also stewards 160,000+ sq ft of nonresidential space including a food shelf, pocket park, neighborhood center
- [champlainhousing.org](https://www.getahome.org/)

**Proud Ground** (Portland, Oregon)
- Founded: 1999
- Scale: 740+ households across 6 counties; 75%+ BIPOC homeowners
- Model: Buyers purchase homes at 20–50% below market, lease land under 99-year agreement
- [proudground.org](https://www.proudground.org)

**Houston Community Land Trust** (Houston, Texas)
- Founded: 2018 (post-Hurricane Harvey)
- Scale: 200+ households; $52.7M city investment in 2021 (one of largest single municipal CLT investments in U.S. history) for ~400 homes
- Model: Partnership with Houston Land Bank; New Home Development Program builds on abandoned properties
- [houstonclt.org](https://www.houstonclt.org/about-us)

---

#### Commercial and Mixed-Use CLT Models (Most Relevant to MCLT)

**Rondo Community Land Trust** (St. Paul, Minnesota)
- Founded: 1993
- The first commercial CLT in Minnesota and one of the most studied commercial CLT models
- Key project: Two mixed-use buildings on Selby Avenue — 34 affordable senior apartments + 9,300 sq ft commercial at ~$7/sq ft (vs. $20–35 market rate)
- Also acquired the Golden Thyme Café, a beloved Black-owned coffee shop, to preserve it when founders retired
- Funded by acquiring city-owned lots at nominal cost
- **Most directly analogous to MCLT's model of anchoring a community commercial asset**
- [rondoclt.org](https://www.rondoclt.org/)

**Crescent City Community Land Trust** (New Orleans, Louisiana)
- Acquires vacant commercial/mixed-use buildings on key commercial corridors
- First major project: Co-development of the historic Pythian building — 69 apartments (25 affordable) + commercial space
- Approach: Reduces blight, creates affordable small business space, supports Black entrepreneurs
- [Brookings case study](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/how-a-new-orleans-community-land-trust-is-providing-permanently-affordable-housing-and-supporting-black-entrepreneurs/)

**Africatown Community Land Trust** (Seattle, Washington)
- Founded: 2016
- Focus: Black Diaspora community preservation in Seattle's Central District
- Holdings: Liberty Bank Building (100+ affordable units + ground-floor retail for Black-owned businesses), Africatown Plaza (130 units + community space + public art)
- [africatownlandtrust.org](https://www.africatownlandtrust.org/)

**Urban Land Conservancy** (Denver, Colorado)
- Founded: 2003
- Model: Combines CLT with private land bank functions; acquires land in gentrifying areas
- Scale: ~300,000 sq ft of commercial and nonprofit space preserved; Dearfield Fund for Black Wealth
- [urbanlandc.org](https://urbanlandc.org/community-land-trust/)

**Partnership in Property Commercial Land Trust** (Minneapolis, Minnesota)
- Founded: 2021 (spun out of City of Lakes CLT pilot begun 2017)
- Focus: Perpetually affordable commercial ownership for BIPOC small business owners
- [pipclt.org](https://www.pipclt.org/)

**City of Lakes Community Land Trust** (Minneapolis, Minnesota)
- Commercial Land Trust Initiative launched ~2017 as pilot
- 400+ low-to-moderate income households in residential portfolio; developed commercial arm
- [clclt.org](https://www.clclt.org/commercial-land-trust-initiative)

**East Harlem/El Barrio Community Land Trust** (New York, New York)
- Founded: 2014
- First NYC CLT to receive public land, capital financing, and startup support from the city
- Focus: Permanently affordable housing + commercial + green + cultural spaces
- Recent milestone: $13M deal transforming 4 city-owned buildings into affordable apartments
- [ehebclt.nyc](https://ehebclt.nyc/)

**Dudley Neighbors Inc.** (Boston, Massachusetts)
- Founded: Late 1980s, from the Dudley Street Neighborhood Initiative
- Unique feature: **Only community-based organization in the U.S. with the power of eminent domain** (granted by the City of Boston)
- 60 acres, 225 units of affordable housing, an urban farm, greenhouse, charter school, parks, town common
- [dudleyneighbors.org](https://www.dudleyneighbors.org/)

---

#### Agricultural CLT Models

**New Communities Inc.** (Albany, Georgia)
- Founded: 1969; the **first CLT in the United States**
- Inspired by Israeli kibbutz model, founded by SNCC civil rights organizers including Rev. Charles Sherrod and Slater King
- Original holding: 5,735 acres near Albany — at the time the largest Black-owned landholding in America
- Land lost in early 1980s due to USDA loan discrimination; received $12.8M settlement in 2009 Pigford v. Glickman case; reestablished
- [newcommunitiesinc.com](https://www.newcommunitiesinc.com/)

**Agrarian Trust / Agrarian Commons** (National, HQ in Maine)
- Founded: ~2015; launched Agrarian Commons initiative in May 2020 with 12 founding farms across 10 states totaling 2,400 acres
- Model: Community-held entities that issue long-term, equitable leases to farmers for regenerative agriculture
- Known Commons: West Virginia Agrarian Commons, Little Jubba Central Maine Agrarian Commons (Somali Bantu community), Southwest Virginia Agrarian Commons, Central Virginia Agrarian Commons, Southeast Minnesota Agrarian Commons
- Focused on next-generation farmers and BIPOC land access as 400M acres of U.S. farmland changes hands over next 15 years
- [agrariantrust.org](https://www.agrariantrust.org/)

**Athens Land Trust** (Athens, Georgia)
- Operated the West Broad Farmers' Market + 1.2-acre West Broad Community Garden + urban farm
- First community-based Certified Naturally Grown urban farm in Athens; produced 13,000+ lbs of produce in first two years; 200+ shoppers/week
- Market went on hiatus in 2025 to find a permanent home — a cautionary tale about the importance of owning vs. borrowing space
- **Highly relevant to MCLT**: demonstrates the value of a CLT owning its farmers market site rather than leasing from a third party
- [athenslandtrust.org](https://athenslandtrust.org/)

**Lopez Community Land Trust** (Lopez Island, Washington)
- Founded: 1989 (after housing costs rose 190% in a single year)
- Hybrid model: 47 cooperatively owned homes (6 rental) + 100 acres of farmland (Stonecrest Farm + Lopez Sound Road Farm & Forest Preserve)
- Net-zero housing; 20+ year sustainable agriculture internship program
- [lopezclt.org](https://www.lopezclt.org/)

**South of the Sound Community Land Trust** (Olympia, Washington)
- Founded: 1997
- 140 acres of farmland preserved, focused on local food systems

**Berkshire Community Land Trust** (Great Barrington, Massachusetts)
- Founded: 1980, by Robert Swann and Susan Witt (co-founders of the Schumacher Center for a New Economics)
- One of the earliest CLTs; designed from the outset for workforce housing, productive farmland, retail sites, appropriately scaled manufacturing sites, and Main Street character properties
- [berkshirecommunitylandtrust.org](https://berkshirecommunitylandtrust.org/)

## Part IV: Common Funding Sources

CLTs use a layered capital stack. The proportions shift depending on whether the initial asset is commercial/agricultural vs. residential:

### Federal Programs
- **Community Development Block Grants (CDBG)** — historically the most common early source
- **HOME Investment Partnerships Program** — affordable housing subsidy
- **SHOP (Self Help Homeownership Opportunity Program)** — sweat equity housing
- **USDA Rural Development programs** — rural CLTs
- **HUD Choice Neighborhoods Initiative** — urban redevelopment
- **ARPA funds** — Harris County, TX committed $15M in 2023; Houston allocated $52.7M in 2021

### State Sources
- State housing trust funds
- State affordable housing finance agencies
- Tax Increment Financing (TIF)
- State brownfield remediation funds (critical to Sawmill CLT's industrial land model)

### Municipal / Local Government
- Donation or sale of city-owned land at nominal cost (critical for Rondo CLT, East Harlem/El Barrio CLT)
- Local housing trust funds
- Predevelopment loan funds (Florida, Colorado, RCAC in 11 western states)
- Metro/regional housing bonds (Portland Metro's $652.8M 2018 bond funded Proud Ground and others)

### CDFI and Mission-Driven Finance
- Community Development Loan Funds and CDFIs (Partners for the Common Good, Low Income Investment Fund, Calvert Impact Capital)
- Revolving loan funds (the Institute for Community Economics pioneered this for CLTs)
- CDFI Fund awards (federal match-required grants)

### Philanthropy
- MacKenzie Scott gave $20M to Champlain Housing Trust (2023)
- Robert Wood Johnson Foundation, Ford Foundation, Kresge Foundation are active funders
- Local community foundations
- Faith-based organizations

### Earned Revenue / Internal
- Ground lease fees (Thistle CLT's $30/month average covers ~1/3 of program costs)
- Membership fees
- Development fees from co-development projects
- Commercial lease income from owned properties

## Part V: CLTs That Started With a Single Commercial or Market Property

This is the question most directly applicable to MCLT's situation (acquiring a 0.84-acre farmers market lot as a founding property).

### 5.1 Rondo CLT — Commercial-First Model (St. Paul, MN)
Founded in 1993 focused on affordable housing, Rondo developed its commercial wing when speculative investors began bidding on lots along the Selby Avenue corridor. Rondo worked with the city to acquire two lots at nominal cost, then co-developed mixed-use buildings with 34 affordable senior apartments on upper floors and 9,300 sq ft of affordable commercial space below at ~$7/sq ft vs. $20–35 market rate. The Golden Thyme Café acquisition shows the model's capacity to steward not just real estate but community anchors. **Lesson for MCLT:** Nominal or below-market city land transfer combined with mission-aligned development partners made commercial CLT development viable.

### 5.2 Athens Land Trust — Farmers Market as Anchor (Athens, GA)
Athens Land Trust operated the West Broad Farmers' Market for years on land it did not own (a school district site), producing over 13,000 lbs of food in its first two years and drawing 200+ weekly shoppers. In 2025, the market went on hiatus specifically because it lacked a permanent home. **Critical lesson for MCLT:** Athens Land Trust demonstrates both the community value of a CLT-anchored farmers market AND the danger of not owning the underlying land. MCLT's instinct to purchase the market lot is validated by this case.

### 5.3 Berkshire Community Land Trust — Mixed-Use from the Start (Great Barrington, MA)
Founded in 1980 by CLT movement pioneers Robert Swann and Susan Witt, BCLT was explicitly designed from day one to hold land for workforce housing, productive farmland, retail sites, and Main Street character properties. **Lesson:** Commercial CLT acquisitions work best when baked into organizational mission from the beginning.

### 5.4 Sawmill CLT — Distressed Site Acquisition as CLT Catalyst (Albuquerque, NM)
Sawmill CLT was formed in 1996 specifically to acquire and remediate a contaminated industrial site that was threatening the neighborhood. The acquisition itself was the founding act of the CLT. From 27 initial acres (growing to 34), Sawmill built 93 affordable homes and 3 rental complexes. **Lesson:** A CLT can be founded around a single opportunity property, with housing/mixed-use development following acquisition.

### 5.5 Partnership in Property CLT — Pilot-to-Independent Model (Minneapolis, MN)
PIP began as a pilot commercial CLT initiative housed within an established housing CLT (City of Lakes CLT) in 2017, studied feasibility, formed an advisory board, and incorporated as an independent nonprofit in 2021. **Lesson:** A nascent commercial CLT can incubate within an established housing CLT's infrastructure, reducing overhead and gaining credibility before standing up independently.

### 5.6 Urban Land Conservancy — Speed and Capital Readiness (Denver, CO)
Denver's ULC has demonstrated that commercial CLTs require a distinct playbook: acquisition of commercial land in strengthening markets demands staff capacity, real estate expertise, and speed. "If capital isn't raised quickly enough, the land becomes too expensive to acquire." **Lesson for MCLT:** Pre-positioning capital (a dedicated acquisition fund or bridge loan) before a specific property is identified is essential to commercial CLT success.

## Part VI: Success Stories, Lessons Learned, and Model Innovations

### 6.1 Key Success Stories

**Champlain Housing Trust — Scale Through Merger:**
Vermont's CHT grew from Burlington Community Land Trust (1984) through a 2006 merger. Today: $223M+ in assets, 150 staff, 3,275 homes, $60M/year in development. Non-residential portfolio includes a food shelf, pocket park, multi-generational center, and elementary school converted to a neighborhood center.

**Dudley Neighbors Inc. — Community Power and Eminent Domain:**
The first and only community-based organization in the U.S. to hold eminent domain authority, DNI assembled 60 acres in Boston's Roxbury neighborhood — enabling a comprehensive community-designed redevelopment rather than a patchwork approach. Multi-ethnic governance model (reserved board seats for Black, Latinx, Cape Verdean, White, and youth residents) has been widely studied.

**Houston CLT — Public Investment at Scale:**
$52.7M from the City of Houston in 2021 to subsidize ~400 homes. Formed post-Hurricane Harvey to resist speculative land grabs in historically Black neighborhoods. Now 200+ households with expansion to tiny homes, ADUs, and repair programs.

**Lopez Community Land Trust — Rural Hybrid Model:**
Founded 1989 after housing costs spiked 190% in one year. Now stewards 47 cooperatively owned homes (several net-zero) AND 100 acres of farmland — demonstrating that a single CLT can serve both residential and agricultural community needs in a Pacific Northwest rural context.

### 6.2 Key Lessons for MCLT

1. **Community organizing must precede or accompany land acquisition.** CLT legitimacy depends on it being a genuine expression of community will. The current signature/pledge campaign is building this foundation.

2. **City land donation is often the difference-maker.** Many successful CLTs received their first land free or at nominal cost from municipalities. Cultivating a City of Portland partnership is worth pursuing in parallel.

3. **Speed and pre-positioned capital are essential for commercial acquisitions.** Unlike housing CLTs, commercial CLT opportunities can evaporate quickly when speculative buyers are competing. The pledge campaign is beginning to build this signal.

4. **Ground lease fees should cover operations over time.** Building a ground lease revenue base creates long-term sustainability beyond grant dependence.

5. **Startup incubation within established CLTs reduces risk.** If a partnership with Proud Ground or another Oregon CLT is possible, it could reduce startup overhead significantly.

6. **26% of active CLTs were incorporated after 2017.** The movement is actively expanding; new CLTs can succeed in the current environment.

## Part VIII: Summary Findings for MCLT's Strategic Planning

**The national CLT movement is large, growing, and diversifying beyond housing.** 308+ CLTs in 48 states is a mature movement with deep institutional knowledge. The West region (37% of CLTs) means MCLT is organizing in the most active CLT geography in the country.

**Commercial CLTs are a small but growing and increasingly celebrated niche.** At least 30 CLTs have commercial portfolios. Rondo CLT, Crescent City CLT, Urban Land Conservancy, Africatown CLT, and Partnership in Property are the leading case studies.

**Starting with a single commercial property is validated by precedent.** Sawmill CLT (single contaminated lot → 34-acre neighborhood), Rondo CLT (two Selby Ave lots → first commercial CLT in Minnesota), Partnership in Property (pilot → independent CLT) — all began with a single or small set of properties.

**Farmers market CLTs are rare but highly validated.** Athens Land Trust's experience is the closest analog: its market produced 13,000 lbs of produce, drew 200+ weekly shoppers, and served as a community anchor — but lacked land ownership and ultimately went on hiatus. MCLT's proposal to acquire the underlying lot directly addresses this vulnerability.

**Oregon's CLT ecosystem is thin but supportive.** With only 4 known Oregon CLTs, MCLT would be a pioneer in the commercial/agricultural CLT space for the state. Proud Ground's track record and the NWCLTC provide a regional support base.

**Funding pathways are well-established.** Most promising near-term sources for MCLT's 0.84-acre acquisition:
1. Portland Metro affordable housing bond ecosystem (which has funded Proud Ground projects)
2. CDBG via Multnomah County
3. CDFI bridge loan for acquisition (Partners for the Common Good has existing OR relationships)
4. Oregon Housing and Community Services state programs
5. Private philanthropy and community capital campaigns
6. Potential City of Portland land use partnership
