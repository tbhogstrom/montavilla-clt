# PCEF 2025 Planning Grants — Analog Analysis for MCLT Application

**Date:** 2026-05-01
**Researcher:** Claude
**Source:** PCEF 2025 Community Grants Funding Awards Report (downloaded to `workspace/grants/pcef/pcef-2025-funding-awards.txt`) + 2024 RFP 3 funding recommendations (`pcef-2024-rfp3-funding-recommendations.txt`)
**Purpose:** Identify the right precedent applications to model the MCLT planning grant on, and correct mischaracterizations in `application-overview.md`

---

## TL;DR — Three Things That Change Our Strategy

1. **The "Albina Vision Trust = our best analog" claim in the brief is misleading.** AVT got $198K, yes, but it is **district-scale clean energy planning for a 10.5-acre redevelopment site that AVT already controls** under their existing Central City Master Plan. Funding area: **Other** (not Regen Ag). It is *not* a community-ownership planning grant or a feasibility study for an unowned site. Useful as a *technical-depth* template, not a scope analog.

2. **The actual best analog is Indigenous Health Equity Institute — "Assessing the Climate Health Impacts of Indigenous Foodways" ($200,000, 1-year, Regenerative Agriculture, ranked #1 in Regen Ag planning).** It includes a "technical feasibility study" deliverable, hit the full $200K cap, and is in our exact funding area. **This is the application to study and emulate.**

3. **All $200K-tier planning grants in the 2025 cycle were 1 year, not 2 years.** The brief's draft scope assumes a 2-year, $200K project. That combination did not fund in 2025. We should either: (a) compress to 1 year at $200K following the precedent, or (b) build the budget at lower $/year for 2 years. The brief's 2-year assumption needs revisiting before we draft.

## #1 ANALOG — Indigenous Health Equity Institute, "Assessing the Climate Health Impacts of Indigenous Foodways"

**$200,000 · 1 year · Regenerative Agriculture · Funding Area Rank #1 · App #9980**

### Verbatim project summary (quoted)

> "This planning project aims to support the refinement, pilot, and community-validated development of the Indigenous Food Sovereignty Health and Wellness Impact Assessment Tool. Key milestones include researching and assessing the impacts of culturally specific foodways on climate resilience and community wellness, hosting community engagement sessions, conducting three locally led food sovereignty assessments, producing a technical feasibility study, and creating a planning report and dashboard. Major goals include integrating climate science indicators and wellness metrics, generating qualitative and quantitative data to evaluate the environmental and health impacts of local food systems, and strengthening climate justice infrastructure led by PCEF priority populations in Portland."

### Why this is our best analog

- **Same funding area:** Regenerative Agriculture, exactly where we plan to apply.
- **Hit the full $200K cap.** Direct evidence the cap is reachable in our category.
- **Ranked #1 within Regen Ag planning grants** — strongest scoring outcome.
- **"Technical feasibility study" is in the deliverable list.** This is exactly the term we plan to use to justify the technical-planning tier. *PCEF awarded the cap to a project where "technical feasibility study" was a deliverable inside a community-engagement-heavy scope.* That validates our framing strategy.
- **Scope structure is a near-template for ours:** community engagement sessions → locally led assessments (3 of them) → technical feasibility study → planning report + dashboard.
- **Deliverable-led structure** (a tool, three assessments, a feasibility study, a report) — concrete artifacts, not just process.

### What we should pull into our application

1. The phrase **"technical feasibility study"** as a named deliverable in our scope and budget. Don't hide it — make it a named line item.
2. **Quantified deliverables:** "three locally led food sovereignty assessments" — concreteness scores. We should commit to specific numbers (e.g., "6 listening sessions, 3 design charrettes, 1 site assessment, 1 financial model, 1 organizational formation deliverable").
3. **Outputs framed as climate justice infrastructure**, not just plans. They strengthen "climate justice infrastructure led by PCEF priority populations" — that exact phrasing is reviewer-ready. Adapt for us: "climate justice infrastructure for Montavilla through community ownership of land."

### What we shouldn't copy

- This project is fundamentally **research/tool development**, not site planning. We have a specific 0.84-acre site; they were producing a tool. Our scope is more concrete and physically grounded — lean into that as a strength.
- Their "Indigenous food sovereignty" framing is theirs; we should not borrow community-identity language that isn't ours.

## #2 ANALOG — Albina Vision Trust, "Planning for Energy, Restoration & Justice in Lower Albina"

**$198,000 · 1 year · Other · Funding Area Rank #1 · App #8898**

### Verbatim project summary (quoted)

> "This project aims to support the development of a district-scale clean energy system within the Albina Vision Trust's Central City Master Plan. The effort will explore integrated thermal, electrical, and renewable energy systems to achieve climate-positive goals for a 10.5-acre redevelopment site. Key milestones include conducting a masterplan energy charrette and visioning sessions to identify sustainability strategies and site constraints, developing concept designs for district-scale energy systems, performing detailed energy and carbon modeling, and preparing cost estimates to align design decisions with feasibility. The project will conclude with a concept-level district design that defines energy sources, infrastructure strategies, and renewable integration. Major goals include advancing a replicable model for equitable, resilient, and carbon-neutral community development."

### Why the brief overrated this as "most analogous"

- **AVT already controls the site.** Their grant is one piece of an active, multi-year master plan with established institutional backing. Ours is a feasibility/community-ownership grant for a privately-owned parcel listed at $5M. Reviewers comparing us to AVT will see asymmetric organizational maturity and site control.
- **Funding area is "Other," not Regen Ag.** The technical scope is energy systems engineering, not land planning or agriculture.
- **No community-ownership or land-acquisition framing.** The "justice" in the title refers to the historical displacement of Albina, addressed through redevelopment — not through community ownership of the land.

### What is genuinely useful here as a template

- **Technical-depth vocabulary.** "Energy charrette," "concept designs," "detailed energy and carbon modeling," "cost estimates to align design decisions with feasibility," "concept-level district design." Steal the *shape* of this language — replace energy systems with site/agriculture/infrastructure systems. This is exactly what "technical planning" sounds like to PCEF reviewers.
- **The 1-year duration at $198K is a calibration point** — confirms the cap-tier planning grant in this cycle ran 1 year.
- **"Replicable model" framing** as a stated major goal. This is a high-scoring move: we should explicitly position MCLT as a replicable East Portland CLT model that other neighborhoods could adopt.

## #3 ANALOG — Neighborhood House, "The Neighborhood House Barber Project"

**$192,690 · 1 year · Energy efficiency · Funding Area Rank #1 · App #9128**

### Why this is the cleanest structural template for our scaffolding

> "Key milestones include hosting community and stakeholder meetings, conducting energy assessments with schematic designs and cost estimates, selecting energy-efficient upgrades based on pricing, climate impact, and community priorities, and presenting the final selection of upgrades with visual boards and walkthroughs, gathering feedback for future implementation and fundraising."

The structural arc is exactly what we need to mirror:

1. **Community + stakeholder meetings** (upfront)
2. **Technical assessments + schematic designs + cost estimates** (middle)
3. **Selection of options based on community priorities** (decision step)
4. **Final presentation gathering feedback for future implementation and fundraising** (close)

That four-step arc translates directly to MCLT:

1. Community + coalition listening sessions, neighborhood demographic and asset mapping
2. Site assessment, infrastructure analysis, zoning/feasibility, BPO, scenario design (community garden / urban farm / food forest / mixed-use / permanent farmers market home)
3. Community-led scenario selection (charrette-driven)
4. Final feasibility report + capital stack + implementation grant readiness

This arc is **reviewer-tested at the $192K tier in 2025** and has the exact equity + community-driven decision-making narrative PCEF wants.

## Implementation Precedent — Prismid Sanctuary (for context, not template)

**$976,800 · 3 years · Regenerative Agriculture · Implementation Grant · App #8830**

> "This project aims to create a climate resilient landscape through the redevelopment of a 0.74-acre parcel in the St. Johns neighborhood to accommodate growing program demand. Key milestones include developing a community-led land design and architectural plans, securing permits and required approvals, securing leveraged funds, completing construction, and providing essential maintenance. Major goals include advancing climate resilience and ecological restoration, promoting PCEF priority population-led land stewardship and agricultural sovereignty, improving access to free, organic, and culturally relevant food, cultivating intergenerational ecological and cultural knowledge-sharing, and the supporting holistic wellness and collective joy."

### Why this matters

Prismid is **what an MCLT implementation grant could look like in ~2027** — a 0.74-acre parcel (we are 0.84-acre), Regen Ag, community-led design, secures construction. They got there through prior planning work and existing organizational maturity. Our 2026 planning grant builds the runway to a Prismid-shaped implementation grant.

**Use Prismid in our narrative as the trajectory:** "We seek the planning grant to position MCLT to apply for a future implementation grant in the model of Prismid Sanctuary." This shows reviewers we understand the PCEF pathway.

### Important difference

Prismid already controlled their parcel. We don't yet. Our planning grant has to build site-control feasibility as one of its deliverables — that's a real difference reviewers will see.

## Strategic Implications for Our Application

### 1. Reconsider the 2-year scope assumption

The brief assumes a 2-year, $200K planning grant. **No 2-year project hit $200K in 2025.** The two 2-year planning grants were $199,997 (Upstream Access — Renewable energy) and $100,000 (Verde — Workforce). Most $200K-tier projects ran 1 year.

**Options:**
- **(a) Tight 1-year, $200K scope** — matches Indigenous Health Equity Institute precedent exactly. Faster path to implementation.
- **(b) 2-year, $200K scope** — possible (Upstream Access did $199,997 over 2 years), but requires extra justification for why our scope can't be done in 1 year.
- **(c) 2-year, ~$140K scope** — closer to the median 2-year planning grant size, but leaves $60K on the table.

**Recommendation:** Default to (a) unless we have a specific reason the work cannot compress. The 2-year assumption was made before precedent data; with the precedent, 1 year is the safer competitive choice.

### 2. The "technical planning" framing is supportable

Indigenous Health Equity Institute hit the $200K cap in our exact category with "technical feasibility study" as one deliverable embedded in a community-engaged scope. **This is direct precedent that the framing works.** Our PCEF question (item 6 in the brief's MUST DO list) should reference this precedent explicitly: *"In the 2025 cycle, the Regen Ag rank-1 planning grant ($200K to Indigenous Health Equity Institute) included a technical feasibility study as a deliverable. We propose a similar structure: technical site, infrastructure, and feasibility analyses with community engagement as the methodology that drives those technical decisions. Does this framing qualify for the $200K technical planning tier?"*

### 3. Deliverables must be concrete and quantified

Every funded planning grant lists specific, countable deliverables. Examples from the awards:
- "Three locally led food sovereignty assessments" (IHEI)
- "Concept-level district design" (AVT)
- "Final selection of upgrades with visual boards and walkthroughs" (Neighborhood House)
- "Pilot program framework" + "summary report with findings, next steps, and cost estimates" (Upstream Access)
- "Feasibility study" + "curriculum" + "capacity-building strategy" (Verde)
- "Final report with recommendations to leadership and funders" (Latino Network)

**Our application should commit to ~6-10 concrete, named deliverables.** Vague phrases like "community planning process" do not score.

### 4. "Replicable model" framing is high-leverage

Both AVT and Indigenous Health Equity Institute used variations of "replicable" as a stated goal. We should explicitly position MCLT as **"a replicable East Portland community land trust model for low-income, environmentally-burdened neighborhoods"** — this maps directly to PCEF's "multiple benefits" guiding principle and signals the grant's value extends beyond one site.

### 5. The Indigenous Foodways application deserves a deeper read

If the full application narrative is publicly available, we should obtain it and study its language and structure. **Action:** Request it from PCEF in the 1-on-1 consultation, or ask Indigenous Health Equity Institute directly if they'll share the application narrative (community-aligned orgs often will).
