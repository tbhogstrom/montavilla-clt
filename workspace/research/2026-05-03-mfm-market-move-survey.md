## Strategic Implications for MCLT

### 1. The "neighborhood institution" case is now MFM's own data

53% walk to the market. 63% live within 1 mile. Community priority #1 is "proximity to home." This is not MCLT's claim — it is MFM's survey finding, published publicly. Use it (citing MFM) in the PCEF narrative, prospectus, media outreach, and any stakeholder conversations.

### 2. Location matters as much as the institution

63% within a mile + #1 community priority (proximity to home) + #4 vendor priority (proximity to current location) = a community-and-vendor mandate to stay at or very near 7700 SE Stark. The survey MFM ran validates the core CLT thesis: this specific piece of land matters to the people who use it.

### 3. The building program answers vendors' top 3 needs

Vendor priorities: (1) drive-up access, (2) year-round capability, (3) level ground. A purpose-built market facility on a CLT-owned site — covered, level, year-round, with vendor loading — is the literal answer to the top three vendor needs. This is leverage for any building-program narrative in the PCEF application.

### 4. Sunday continuity is a real asset

~40% of vendors say they couldn't attend on Saturday. Sunday is not interchangeable. The campaign should not treat the market day as a free variable in any scenario planning.

### 5. Bike accessibility (#3 community) intersects with PCEF climate framing

Bike access as a top-3 community priority connects directly to PCEF's clean energy / active-transportation mandate. Foreground this in the planning-grant narrative: a walkable, bikeable market site reduces vehicle trips — and the survey proves the community already prioritizes this.

### 6. Transit is mid-pack, not dominant

Public transportation accessibility ranks #6 — the community's access pattern is walk/bike/short-car, not transit-dependent. Useful for framing but not a lead argument.
